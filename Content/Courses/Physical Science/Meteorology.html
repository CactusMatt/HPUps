2.   Purpose/Objectives/Assessment

 

A.         Purpose statement.

 

This course is designed as an exploration of the basic physical concepts and principles associated with meteorology with an emphasis on the way scientific theories are developed in meteorology. The course provides a foundation for understanding the importance of science and modern technology. Specifically, the student should gain an enhanced appreciation of weather works, develop a greater awareness of natural events that contribute to the weather, and become more scientifically and technologically literate. The course will be directed toward non-science majors needing a science course which has both lecture and lab components to provide both experience and knowledge.

 

B.         Intended Outcomes for the course

 

Students will view physical sciences in a more positive, less intimidating way.  The student will be able to explain basic meteorology concepts.  The student will demonstrate basic mathematical skills and the use of the scientific method of investigation. The student will be skilled at integrating technology into scientific discovery and presentation—e-mail, Internet, word processing, Excel, Power point, digital cameras, etc.  The student will be confident enough in their own physical science knowledge that he or she can reasonably determine good science from poor science when seen in the news, on the internet or TV, brought up in social conversations or when needed in deciding voting issues or jury duty decisions.

C.   Overall teaching methodology.

 

This course will involve a variety of materials and instructional strategies such as “group learning”, student lead instruction and other experiences that will enhance the students’ skills.  Technologically, we will also try posting information on-line and have them send information via e-mail. Students will be tested on the concepts learned.  Still, there will be many hands on experiences through classroom activities, technology and laboratory experiences. Students will use a variety of teaching methods which are flexible, actively engage the students in learning, and provide the students with ideas they can use later.  The lab activities will utilize simple and readily available supplies and materials that emphasize physical science. The course is to introduce students to physical science concepts and give them an appreciation for science.

 

D.   Practice beyond scheduled lab or class time.

 

Keeping current with material is an essential activity. Students should come to the lectures having read carefully the appropriate material in the text at least once.  Students should come to lab having read the lab instructions and completed the Pre-lab handout.  Students should spend at least eight hours per week outside of class studying, reading the text, doing homework assignments, working on pre- and post-labs, working on the weekly report and discussing physical science with classmates and the instructor.

 

E.   Assistance available:  office hours, tutors, learning labs or other centers.

 

If a student needs help, ASK!  The student is urged to come by during office hours.  If the student stops by other times and no one is there, leave a note, or use email (email is the most effective means to contact me outside of office hours). If the posted office schedule does not fit the student’s schedule, the student should arrange an appointment. The instructor’s job is to assist the student’s learning.  If difficulties arise that lectures and labs do not seem to clear up, the student should take the initiative and see the instructor.

C.   Attendance requirements and effect upon grade.

 

Students are expected to attend all lectures and labs.  The lecture and lab will require active involvement. The student is responsible and accountable for the information presented and the activities carried out during lecture and lab time. The students are expected to stay the entire two hours of the scheduled lab.  The student must notify the instructor 48 hours in advance if they are unable to attend a lab or exam. If you miss 11 times, you automatically fail the class, no exceptions. Also, excused or unexcused absences both count toward the 11 absences. The only difference is that work missed for an excused absence can be made up, but not for unexcused absences. Wet labs cannot be made up due to setup, lab room availability, and safety restrictions.
